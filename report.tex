\subsection*{1.1 Appreciation of BJT as an Amplifier}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{1.1 Appreciation of BJT as an Amplifier}

A \textbf{Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT)} is a three-terminal semiconductor device consisting of two p-n junctions. For an NPN transistor, it has three terminals: The Base(B), Collector (C), and Emitter (E). Because of the three terminals, it has the following currents and voltage drops:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Collector voltage $v_{c}$
    \item Emitter voltage $v_{e}$
    \item Base voltage $v_{b}$
    \item Collector current $i_{c}$
    \item Emitter current $i_{e}$
    \item Base current $i_{b}$
\end{itemize}

\begin{circuitikz}[american voltages, american currents]
    % Draw the NPN transistor
    \draw (0,0) node[npn] (Q) {};
    
    % Base terminal, voltage label, and current
    \draw (-2,0) node[anchor=east] {$v_b$} to[short, o-, i=$i_b$] (Q.B);
    
    % Collector terminal, voltage label, and current
    \draw (Q.C) ++(0,1.5) node[anchor=south] {$v_c$} to[short, o-, i=$i_c$] (Q.C);
    
    % Emitter terminal, current, and voltage label
    \draw (Q.E) to[short, i=$i_e$, -o] ++(0,-1.5) node[anchor=north] {$v_e$};
    
\end{circuitikz}

The fundamental property that makes a BJT useful as an amplifier is how the output current gets multiplied from the input current by a constant $\beta$:

\begin{equation}
    I_C = \beta \, I_B, \qquad I_E = I_C + I_B = (1+\beta)\,I_B
\end{equation}

Say an AC current is fed into the base. The transistor will multiply this current by $\beta$ at the collector. This large collector current then flows through a resistor $R_C$, which creates the voltage drop $v_{out}$ = $i_c R_C$. As the input voltage varies, the output voltage varies too, resulting in voltage amplification.

For a BJT to function normally as an amplifier, it must operate in the active region ($V_{CE} \ge V_{CE(sat)}$. A voltage divider bias network is often used to maintain the BJT inside the active region by modifying the values of $R_1$ and $R_2$.

\subsection*{1.3 Power Dissipation Formula}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{1.3 Power Dissipation Formula}
For power dissipation, we will use the total power formula for the circuit (as hinted in the assignment instructions, in which we are given the formula that $I_{total}$ = $\sum_{n=1,2,3 \cdots} I_n$). 

We know that power equals to voltage times current, and since the current hinted to be used is $I_{total}$, the voltage must be $V_{total}$ (In this case, it is $V_{CC}$). 

\begin{align*}
    \boxed{P_{total} = V_{cc} I_{total}}
\end{align*}

A key observation to point out here is that $I_C$ is most influential in $I_{total}$ for all the three circuits. Hence, $I_C$ can be said to directly alter the power dissipation. This observation is the key in determining circuit sustainability during the analysis later in this report.

\subsection*{1.4 Voltage Swing Formula}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{1.4 Voltage Swing Formula}
From research, voltage swing is just the peak-to-peak output voltage. But in order to calculate it theoretically, we will need to know what conditions of the circuit will result in the output voltage going to the extreme peak. From research, we found that a transistor can swing betwen two extremes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cutoff: Where $I_C$ approaches 0, and $I_B$ approaches 0. Basically, the transistor is assumed to be "open circuit".

\end{itemize}

Then, from research, the peak output voltage $v_{out}$ is given as:
\[
v_{out} = \min\!\left(\left|V_{CE} - V_{C(sat)}\right|,\; \left|V_{C(cutoff)} - V_{CE}\right|\right)
\]

And since our voltage swing is the peak to peak, we will have to multiply $v_{out}$ by 2:
\[
    \boxed{V_{swing} = 2 \times v_{out}}
\]

It is important to note here that a well-designed amplifier is the one that places the $V_{CE}$ at the midpoint between saturation and cutoff, hence maximising $V_{swing}$. And generally, the lower the $V_{CE}$, the lower the $V_{swing}$, because low $V_{CE}$ means the transistor is closer to saturation, which limits the voltage swing from the other end.

\subsection*{1.5 Bandwidth Formula}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{1.5 Bandwidth Formula}

Based on research, the bandwidth is the range of input frequencies in which the input can be amplified. From research the formula for bandwidth is:
\[
    \boxed{BW = f_H - f_L}
\]

where $f_H$ is the \textbf{upper} ($-3\,\text{dB}$) cutoff frequency and $f_L$ is the \textbf{lower} ($-3\,\text{dB}$) cutoff frequency.

\subsubsection*{Lower Cutoff Frequency $f_L$}

Theoretically, $f_L$ is determined by the sum of all pole frequencies from the available capacitors in the circuit ($C_{in}$, $C_C$, $C_E$). Recall the formula for the frequency of capacitors.
\[
    f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi Z_C}
\]
There are three such capacitors in the circuit:
\begin{align*}
    f_{c_{in}} &= \frac{1}{2\pi C_{in} \cdot Z_{in}} & \text{(input coupling pole)} \\
    f_{c_C}    &= \frac{1}{2\pi C_C \cdot Z_{C}}      & \text{(output coupling pole)} \\
    f_{c_E}    &= \frac{1}{2\pi C_E \cdot Z_{E}}         & \text{(emitter bypass pole)} \\ \\
    f_L &= f_{c_{in}} + f_{c_C} + f_{c_E}
\end{align*}

\subsubsection*{Upper Cutoff Frequency $f_H$}

In order to calculate $f_H$, we need to know the capacitors \textbf{inside} the transistor itself. (Turns out, inside the BJT we have two capacitors). One of them is called $C_\pi$ which is between the base and emitter, and also $C_\mu$ which is between base and collector.

from research, $f_H$ is the minimum of $f_{Hi}$ and $f_{Ho}$.

$f_{Hi}$ has the formula of:
\[
    f_{Hi} = \frac{1}{2\pi \, R_{th(in)} \cdot C_{in(tot)}}, \qquad C_{in(tot)} = C_\pi + C_{Mi}
\]

Where $C_{Mi}$ is the Miller input capacitance:
\[
    C_{Mi} = C_\mu (1 - A_v) \approx C_\mu (1 + |A_v|) 
\]

$f_{Ho}$ has the formula of:
\[
    f_{Ho} = \frac{1}{2\pi \, R_{th(out)} \cdot C_{out(tot)}}, \qquad C_{out(tot)} = C_{Mo}
\]

Where $C_{Mo}$ is the Miller output capacitance:
\[
    C_{Mo} = C_\mu (1 - \frac{1}{A_v})
\]

\[
    \boxed{f_H = \min(f_{Hi}, f_{Ho})}
\]

Even though the theoretical calculation of BW seemed quite intimidating and unintuitive, thankfully there's a nice visual representation of what BW means. It doesn't really help much in the theoretical calculation, but it is quite useful to understand BW. Basically, if we plot a graph of voltage gain vs frequency, where the voltage gain is in dB (dB = $20\log_{10}(A_v)$), the graph will rise and then become flat and then drop. Then, the BW is basically the length of region where the graph is flat, with a 3dB tolerance. Technically, it is the difference between the upper and lower cutoff frequencies, $f_H$ and $f_L$, which are the frequencies where the voltage gain drops by 3dB from the maximum voltage gain.

Looking at the detailed diagram for Circuit A, the collector current ($I_C$) acts as the primary driver. When an external variable (like $V_{CC}$) forces $I_C$ to increase, it causes Power Dissipation ($P_{total}$) to rise with it (a direct relationship). However, this same increase in $I_C$ suppresses Gain, Bandwidth, Voltage Swing, and Impedance. Because Power increases while those four metrics decrease, Power shares an inverse trade-off (red dashed line) with all of them in the simplified polygon. Conversely, because Gain, Bandwidth, Voltage Swing, and Impedance are all pushed down together by $I_C$, they move in unison, meaning they share direct relationships (blue solid lines) with one another.

\paragraph{Circuit A: }
Circuit A is properly biased in the active region with $V_{CE} = 5.254$\,V, which sits perfectly near the middle of the load line ($V_{CC}/2 = 5$\,V). This provides excellent symmetrical voltage swing. The collector current is 2.366\,mA. The total power drawn from the supply is approximately 30\,mW ($I_{total} \approx 3.028$\,mA).

\paragraph{Circuit B}
Circuit B is also well-biased in the active region with $V_{CE} = 2.959$\,V. The base voltage is $V_B = 5.371$\,V. The emitter degeneration is effectively split ($R_{E1}=R_{E2}=1$\,k$\Omega$), and the collector current is highly similar to Circuit A at $I_C = 2.337$\,mA. Just like Circuit A, the bias resistors were selected to ensure the total supply current matches closely ($I_{total} \approx 2.999$\,mA), meaning its overall power dissipation is practically identical to Circuit A.

\paragraph{Circuit C}
Circuit C is now properly biased into the active region ($V_{CE} = 2.488$\,V). The collector current is lower ($I_C = 1.037$\,mA), supported by the PNP active load mirror. Because of the sophisticated active load, the bias network could be designed with larger resistors ($R_1 = 8.3$\,k$\Omega$, $R_5 = 9.3$\,k$\Omega$), allowing the circuit to maintain a consistent overall supply current ($I_{total} \approx 3.036$\,mA) that perfectly matches the $\approx$ 30\,mW total power constraint of the other two circuits.

\subsubsection*{5.1.2 AC Performance Trade-offs}
Using the small signal T model, we managed to derive various mathematical equations describing different attributes of the circuit in terms of controllable variables (R, C, etc). A simulation website is developed to analyze the relationship between the derived attributes, and a performance polygon is created based on the analysis. The following is the summary of the entire analysis finding.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Qualitative AC performance ranking across the three topologies (H = High, M = Medium, L = Low, Same = Identical).}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Circuit A} & \textbf{Circuit B} & \textbf{Circuit C} \\
        \midrule
        Voltage Gain $|A_v|$ & H & L & H \\
        Bandwidth $BW$       & M & H & L \\
        Input Impedance $Z_{in}$ & L & H & L \\
        Output Impedance $Z_{out}$ & M & M & H \\

        Gain--Bandwidth Stability & L & H & M \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \label{tab:ac_comparison}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Voltage Gain:}
Circuit A achieves high gain (32.96\,dB) because $A_v \approx -\frac{\alpha}{r_e}(R_C \| R_L)$ and $r_e$ is very small when $I_C$ is large.
Circuit B intentionally trades gain for stability and bandwidth using $R_{E1}$, which appears in the denominator of the gain equation. This degeneration is so severe that it actually attenuates the signal ($-0.57$\,dB). Circuit C replaces $R_C$ with an active load mirror, which drives the voltage gain very high (31.82\,dB) even with a smaller $I_C$.

\paragraph{Bandwidth:}
Because Circuit B heavily degenerates its gain, it achieves a massive bandwidth extension (65.75\,MHz). Circuit A sits in the middle (39.8\,MHz), while Circuit C has the narrowest bandwidth (8.52\,MHz). This clearly demonstrates the fundamental gain--bandwidth product trade-off inherent in amplifier design.

\paragraph{Input Impedance.}
$Z_{in} = R_{TH} \| (1+\beta)r_e$ for Circuit A.
Because $r_e = V_T / I_E$ and $I_E$ is large, $r_e$ is small, making $Z_{in}$ small.
Circuit B adds $R_{E1}$ in series which significantly increases its $Z_{in} \approx R_{TH} \| (1+\beta)(r_e + R_{E1})$. This means that Circuit B does not load the signal source nearly as much as Circuit A.

\paragraph{Output Impedance:}
Both Circuit A and Circuit B have the same moderate output impedance ($Z_{out} \approx R_C = 1\,\text{k}\Omega$). In contrast, Circuit C has a very \textbf{high} intrinsic output impedance because an active load current mirror acts as a high-impedance current source ($Z_{out} \approx r_{o1} \| r_{o2}$). Because of this high output impedance, Circuit C is highly susceptible to loading effects and typically requires a buffer stage (like an emitter follower) to successfully drive low-resistance loads without a massive voltage drop.

\subsubsection*{5.1.3 Metric Polygon Observations}
A few key observations from the metric polygon:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$I_C$ is the central driver:} Changing almost any external variable will change $I_C$, and $I_C$ in turn changes almost all the other performance metric variables. This single observation explains almost all of the trade-off structure seen in all three circuits.

    \item \textbf{$C_E, C_{in}, C_C$ are free parameters for bandwidth.} Increasing $C_E, C_{in}, C_C$ improves $BW$ in all three circuits without affecting $A_v$, $Z_{in}$, or $Z_{out}$.

    \item \textbf{Emitter Degeneration Extends Bandwidth.} The defining feature of Circuit B is its emitter degeneration resistor ($R_{E1}$). By providing strong negative feedback, it dramatically suppresses voltage gain but proportionally extends the bandwidth, allowing it to achieve the highest operating frequency of all three designs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{5.2 Sustainable Design Analysis}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{5.2 Sustainable Design Analysis}

In electronics, sustainable design means choosing circuit designs that minimise wasted power, reduce heat generation, extend component life time, and keep the design functional across a wide range of operating conditions.

\subsubsection*{5.2.1 Power Efficiency}

\begin{itemize}
    \item For overall power efficiency, \textbf{all three circuits} have been carefully matched to draw nearly identical total supply current ($I_{total} \approx 3.0$\,mA) from the 10\,V source. This ensures their total power dissipation ($P_{total} = V_{CC} \times I_{total}$) is practically identical at approximately 30\,mW. Hence, the power efficiency is not the defining factor of sustainable design in this scenario.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{5.2.2 Thermal Stability and Reliability}

To analyze temperature, we need to look at the power dissipated inside the transistor instead of the power used up by the entire circuit. Hence, we need to look at $P_{transistor}$, which is $V_{CE}$ times $I_C$.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Circuit A} dissipates the most heat at the Q1 junction (12.43\,mW) because its $V_{CE}$ is the highest.
    \item \textbf{Circuit B} reduces this dissipation to 6.915\,mW by dropping some of the excess voltage across the emitter degeneration resistors rather than across the transistor junction itself.
    \item \textbf{Circuit C} dissipates the least transistor heat (2.580\,mW) because it operates at a much lower $I_C$, proving to be the most thermally efficient and reliable design for the primary transistor.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{5.2.3 Component Count and Complexity}

Sustainable design also factors in how many components are used. In this case, Circuit A uses 8 components, Circuit B uses one additional resistor, while Circuit C adds two more PNP transistors, which makes it significantly more complex (obviously seen in the DC and AC analysis of circuit C).

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Circuit B} achieves its improved stability and sustainability by only the cost of one extra resistor, which is an excellent trade off.
    \item \textbf{Circuit C}, although dissipates the least heat while obtaining a high voltage gain and good voltage swing, it adds two extra transistors compared to A and B, making it the most complex and least sustainable design.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{5.3 Conclusion}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{5.3 Conclusion}

This study compared three CE amplifier configurations --- passive resistive (A), emitter degeneration (B), and active loading (C) --- through theoretical derivation, computer-aided metric-polygon simulation, and PSpice verification.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item From the analysis above, it can be seen that from a purely sustainability standpoint, \textbf{Circuit B is the most sustainable design} because under the same power dissipation, it provides strong voltage gain and bandwidth, maintaining good thermal stability, while only costing one additional resistor compared to Circuit A.

    \item \textbf{Circuit C delivers the best performance} in terms of voltage swing, voltage gain, and thermal stability, but at the cost of high transistor count. Hence, it is only suitable for high-performance devices that doesn't factor in circuit complexity and component costs.

    \item \textbf{Circuit A is the simplest baseline}. It provides the best gain with the fewest parts, but offers low thermal stability.
\end{enumerate}
